R Statistics T-test

September 16, 2017
Statistics Functions

Ah t-tests, A t-test is normally an analysis of two populations means through the use of statistical examination; a t-test with two samples is commonly used with small sample sizes, testing the difference between the samples when the variances of two normal distributions are not known. However, there is such a thing as a one sample t-test. The one sample t-test is a statistical procedure used to determine whether a sample of observations could have been generated by a process with a specific mean. For more information I found a pretty explainative article here: http://www.statisticssolutions.com/manova-analysis-one-sample-t-test/

Anyway you can actually perform a one sample t-test three different ways in R. The first way uses standard deviation, mean, square root, and the quantile function. The quantile function allows you to account for a confidence interval. If you read the article you understand why this is valuable. Any way if you look at the code below you can see an output of a range of values that your proposed mean can fall into. For this example we will use a mean of 4.2 and a confidence interval of 95%

sticklengths<- c( 6.08, 3.86, 2.28, 2.92, 3.21, 3.52, 4.45, 2.30, 2.83, 2.24)
lowerbnd<- mean(sticklengths) - qt(.975,9)* sd(sticklengths) /sqrt(10)
upprbnd<- mean(sticklengths) + qt(.975,9)* sd(sticklengths) /sqrt(10)

upprbnd
## [1] 4.225528
lowerbnd
## [1] 2.512472

We can clearly see that 4.2 barely falls within that range. Another way to calculate this is through the use of Râ€™s t.test function. See below:

t.test(sticklengths,conf.level = 0.95)
## 
##  One Sample t-test
## 
## data:  sticklengths
## t = 8.8978, df = 9, p-value = 9.373e-06
## alternative hypothesis: true mean is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  2.512472 4.225528
## sample estimates:
## mean of x 
##     3.369

One can also identify this range by specifiying your proposed mean. See Below:

t.test(sticklengths,conf.level = 0.95, mu = 4.2)
## 
##  One Sample t-test
## 
## data:  sticklengths
## t = -2.1947, df = 9, p-value = 0.05582
## alternative hypothesis: true mean is not equal to 4.2
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  2.512472 4.225528
## sample estimates:
## mean of x 
##     3.369

So all in all not to bad, remember this is basic statistics, but the theory it is founded in can be complicated. I highly recommend reading the sourced article. If your still interested in R please keep looking at other posts on this blog.
